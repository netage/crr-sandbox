Introduction

Modern fire service agencies engage in many activities beyond responding to fires or even other types of emergencies. Many fire service agencies actively engage fire prevention and other community risk reduction ("CRR") activities as part of their comprehensive, all-hazards mitigation and response, capabilities. While many fire service agencies conduct these types of activities, there appears to be little consistency in how these activities are documented or what types of data are created.

Audience

The overall goal of this project is to develop a prototype data model. The National Fire Data System being developed by NFPA is expanded for quality local and national data for CRR decision making. The project will convene subject matter experts from the enforcer, public education, CRR, codes & standards, and data communities to identify current best practices and existing data formats to develop a scalable, flexible, CRR data model.

CoSAT

CoSAT

Stakeholder Poudre Fire Authority, Colorado
Stakeholder type Fire Department
Source/contact Michael Durkin, Education Specialist

What is the CRR project goal? When is your project successful?

CoSAT Program's goal is to combine home fire safety and provide social help for low-income households. Success:

  • Number of alarms installed
  • The number of needs identified and met
  • The number of hazards identified and mitigated
CoSAT targets small pockets of neglected residents-in this case mobile home parks, which they noted are often known as “resource deserts”. To deal with this small sample size; CoSAT uses a concept of micro-communities, which emphasizes community perceptions in addition to call data. The strong anecdotal evidence from the communities is that this program made them feel safer in their homes, increased their knowledge, and built their trust in the fire department. CoSAT volunteers reported all feedback. The call data, although limited, supports the anecdotal data. 12.4% of homes canvassed made an emergency call in the 150 days after a CoSAT event and 10% of those homes suffered a fire. Of homes that used CoSAT services, only 1.1% made an emergency call and zero of those were fires.

How is the project organized?

The pre-event formative evaluation was a geographic, qualitative smoke-alarm targeting. We knew the targeted homes were older and in low-income areas. The mobile home parks targeted exist outside city limits which mean fewer resources are available to these residents. The age of homes and their place in a resource-desert meant that very few of these homes would have working alarms. Low-income households often lack the means to get these without aid. This pre-event evaluation identified nine parks, comprising 266 homes. During these events, social work students from Colorado State University's School of Social Work conducted an informal, in-person assessment of the residents in the home. Their task was to identify needs the resident has, beyond smoke alarms. Rather than applying a limiting model to this conversation, we allowed the residents to self-identify their biggest need(s). This second formative evaluation guided our follow-up efforts with these residents.

Which CRR activity/activities do you employ?

The Community Service Action Teams (CoSAT) program do house visits. First visit:

  1. Install smoke alarm
  2. Give fire safety instructions
  3. Identify resident needs
  4. Inform on help initiatives. All CoSAT members are debriefed after each event to guide future events. They identified a need for more in-home resources. Second visit within 2 weeks.
  5. Meet needs identified. CoSAT responds to needs identified by the community. As such, it is undergoing constant evaluation. As needs come from the community, we identify local resources to meet them. Some identified needs included winter clothing, food assistance, utility assistance, and employment help. These resources are blended into future CoSAT events, resulting in an ever-growing resource-bank. All CoSAT members are debriefed after each event to guide future events. They identified a need for more in-home resources. At the first event, they were given a home-assessment form to get basic contact information and record needs. As our resource bank grew, CoSAT teams now carry backpacks of brochures and other materials.

Which data are used to target this CRR group?

  1. Older homes
  2. Low income households
  3. Mobile home parks outside city limits

Which CRR activities should be registered?

  1. CoSAT members
  2. Date
  3. Address
  4. Number of smoke alarms installed
  5. Fire Safety instruction given?
  6. Identified needs
  7. Which needs are met? How?
  8. What information is shared?
  9. Which hazards are identified?
  10. Which hazard is mitigated? How?
  11. Debriefing - learnings from this event

Causal Chain Analysis

Stakeholder Lebanon Fire Department in Pittsburgh, PA
Stakeholder type Fire Department
Source/contact Christopher Switala, Education Specialist

What is the CRR project goal? When is your project successful?

Causal Chain Analysis project addressed 2 goals:

  1. Change occupants behavior in a fire by
    1. Get alarmed by installed smoke alarms
    2. Do not attempt to fight the fire
    3. Get out and call 911
  2. Prevent fires by blowing out candles when leaving a room

Success is measured by:

  1. Increased number of homes involved in structure fires had a working smoke alarm
  2. Reduction of the percentage of candle fires
  3. Reduction of the percentage of residential structure fires where the occupant attempted to extinguish the fire
  4. Reduction of the percentage of residential structure fires where the occupant attempted to extinguish the fire
  5. Reduction of the percentage of cooking fires where the occupant attempted to extinguish the fire
  6. Reduction of the civilian fire-related injuries
  7. Increase of buy-in by line personnel for CRR efforts (due to visible results)
  8. Reduction in the number of residential structure fires and associated dollar loss
  9. Reduction in the dollar loss per structure fire
  10. Reduction of number of fatalities

How is the project organized?

Analysis of five years of incident data and circumstances was conducted to identify the prevalent root causes for fire related risk reduction strategies. Causal chain analysis based on incident data was used to identify the prevalent root causes for fire-related risk reduction strategies. Interventions addressing these three areas were:

  1. Reduce number of occupants that attempt to fight the fire
  2. Increase number of working smoke alarms
  3. Decrease number of candle fires

Which CRR activity/activities do you employ?

Messaging developed “Get out and call 911” & “blow out candles when leaving a room”.

    1. Targeted wards
    2. Post-fire neighborhood canvassing
  2. Messaging on community magazine, social media posts
  3. Incorporate message into the elementary school fire and life safety education program

Youth-Set Fires

Fire Safe Cooking

Stakeholder Minnesota Department of public safety
Stakeholder type State Department
Source/contact Kathy Osmonson, Deputy State Fire Marshal

What is the CRR project goal? When is your project successful?

Reduce number of youth-set fires and the underlying problems. Success:

  1. Reduce number of youth-set fires
  2. Provide intervention to all youth who are involved in starting fires

How is the project organized?

Minnesota Department of Public Safety's State Fire Marshal Division has reported on Youth-Set Fires. The purpose for identifying these children is to offer intervention services so that they will learn the dangers of setting fires and stop the behavior. Minnesota has a statewide network of regional youth fire intervention specialists. When a child is referred to one of the regions, the regional program manager initiates contact with the family. A youth fire intervention specialist visits with the family and uses a standard actuarial (answers are numerically weighted) assessment tool to determine the child's intervention needs. The tool provides intervention direction for the high and low scoring kids. High scoring children will often be referred to mental health care. Low scoring children may simply need an educational intervention consisting of intensive fire science, prevention and safety topics. Even children who start cooking, smoking or fireworks fires will benefit from fire education.

Which CRR activity/activities do you employ?

  1. National Fire Academy: Youth firesetting Prevention and Intervention Courses
  2. Iowa's Juvenile Fire Intervention Program
  3. YFIRES, The Youth Firesetting Information Repository & Evaluation System
  4. FIREFACTOR - a Colorado springs fire department prevention program
  5. Provide education sessions to brief residents on the project
  6. Minnesota Model of Youth Fire Intervention

  3. Race
  4. Grade in school at the time of the incident
  5. Family type of youth
  6. Zip code
  7. Primary caregiver's highest level of education
  8. Referral to program initiated by
  9. Has the youth had previous firesetting incidents (If yes - #10, “how many times” is activated)
  10. Incident month (auto-populated from the Incident Date)
  11. Incident year (auto-populated from the Incident Date)
  12. Incident day of the week
  13. Incident time of day
  14. Incident location
  15. Ignition source
  16. First item ignited
  17. Were there associates involved in the incident
  18. Final disposition of case
  19. Was an incident report entered into the National Fire Incident Reporting System (NFIRS)

Which CRR activities should be registered?

The activities to be documented are per case. It helps in the event that the individual starts another fire, then the YFPI Specialist can look back and see what has already been done.
  1. Intervention strategies used
  2. Follow-up information about behavior change and recidivism
